When to Use It — and When Not To

Purpose: Turn the strengths and limitations into a practical decision framework. When does it make sense to reach for generative AI, and when should you leave it alone?
Use it when:
  • You need a first draft, not a final product. It's excellent at getting something on the page — a structure, a starting point, a rough version you can shape. The value is in the time saved getting from a blank page to a working draft.
  • The task is about language and structure. Summarising, reformatting, translating, adjusting tone, reorganising content — these play directly to its strengths. It's working with patterns it knows well.
  • You can verify the output. If you have the knowledge to read the result and spot what's wrong, you're in a good position. The tool does the heavy lifting; you do the quality control.
  • You want to think through options. It's a useful sounding board — "give me five ways I could approach this" or "what am I missing here?" It won't always be right, but it gives you material to react to, which is often more useful than staring at a blank page.
  • The stakes are low to moderate. Internal planning, brainstorming, early-stage drafts, personal productivity — if a mistake in the output is easy to catch and cheap to fix, the risk is well matched.
Don't use it when:
  • You can't check whether the output is correct. If you don't have the expertise to verify the result, you have no way of knowing whether it's useful or dangerously wrong. This is the single biggest risk.
  • The task involves personal or sensitive data. What you type into an AI tool may be stored or used for training (see Section 6 for detail). If the information is confidential, private, or sensitive, don't include it.
  • The stakes are high, and the output is used straight away. Legal documents, medical advice, financial decisions, high-stakes communications — anywhere a plausible-sounding error could cause real harm. The tool doesn't know the difference between a harmless mistake and a consequential one.
  • You need guaranteed accuracy. It doesn't look things up. It doesn't verify. If you need a fact, a citation, a regulation, or a number to be correct, go to the source. Don't trust the AI to get it right just because the answer sounds confident.
  • The task requires judgment about people. Hiring decisions, performance assessments, and learning support recommendations — these require human understanding of context, relationships, and consequences that AI simply doesn't have.
Key Landing Point

The decision isn't "should I use AI or not?" — it's "does this task match what AI is good at, and can I manage the limitations?" If you can verify the output and the stakes allow for imperfection, it's probably a good fit. If you can't check it or the cost of error is high, keep a human in the driver's seat.

Activities based on Section 4

Provide a list of all the strengths and limitations of AI, potentially in a table, then ask participants to write down the implications of each of these for their work.
