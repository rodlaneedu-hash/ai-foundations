What It's Good At — and Where It Falls Over

Purpose: Now that the audience knows how it works, strengths and limitations are logical consequences — not a list to memorise.

The framing: every strength has a flip side. Same mechanics, different outcome depending on how you use it.

It's fast and scalable

It can draft, summarise, translate, or restructure content in seconds. That's genuinely useful.

The flip side

Fast doesn't mean right. Speed comes from predicting the next word — not from checking whether the output is accurate.

It's versatile

It can work across writing, planning, analysis, code, and brainstorming — because it was trained on text spanning many domains.

The flip side

Broad doesn't mean deep. It's only as good as the patterns in its training data for any given topic. It can sound authoritative on a subject for which it has very little useful training data. And critically, it knows nothing about you — your organisation, your role, your industry, your students, your clients, your specific context. Unless you provide that context in your prompt, every response is generic. This is why the quality of your prompt matters so much: the AI can only work with what you give it.

It's fluent

It produces natural, well-structured language. This is what it's best at — after all, language patterns are exactly what it was trained on.

The flip side

Sounding right and being right are completely unrelated. A confidently written paragraph full of errors looks identical to a confidently written paragraph that's accurate. You can't tell the difference by reading the tone.

It's always available

It doesn't get tired, it doesn't have a bad day, and there's no wait time.

The flip side

It has no judgment. It can't tell when it's out of its depth. It won't say "I don't know enough about this to help you" — it'll just produce something plausible and move on.

It recombines ideas in useful ways

Because it's been trained on such a wide range of text, it can make unexpected connections — suggesting approaches you might not have considered.

The flip side

It's mixing and matching existing ideas, not coming up with new ones. It's remixing patterns, not inventing. There's no intent or understanding behind the combinations.

It adapts within a conversation

You can give it feedback, and it adjusts — change the tone, restructure the format, go deeper on one section. It uses everything you've said so far in the conversation to shape its responses.

The flip side

That learning disappears when the conversation ends. Next time you start a chat, it has no memory of what you discussed. It starts fresh every time (unless the tool has been specifically built to remember).

It can work with a lot of information at once

You can paste in long documents, data, meeting notes, or policies and ask it to summarise, analyse, or restructure them. Most modern tools can handle tens of thousands of words in a single conversation.

The flip side

There's a ceiling. Every tool has a limit on how much text it can keep track of at once — your input, its output, and the full back-and-forth history of the conversation all count towards that limit. If you paste in a very long document or have a very long conversation, it quietly forgets the earliest parts of the conversation — and it won't tell you it's happened. If output quality starts dropping partway through a long session, this is often why.

Key Landing Point

The strengths are real — this is a genuinely useful tool. The limitations aren't bugs that will be fixed in the next update. They're baked into the technology's design. Using it well means leaning into what it's good at while designing around where it falls over.
