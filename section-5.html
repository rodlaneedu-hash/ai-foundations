Three Ways to Use Generative AI

Purpose: The bridge from understanding to doing. Once you've decided AI fits the task, this framework helps you choose the right kind of engagement. Drawn from Anthropic's AI Fluency training.
Mode 1: Automation (Task Execution) "Do this for me."

You give the AI a clear, specific instruction. It completes the task. You check the result.

This is the most common starting point. The task is well-defined, the output is predictable, and your role is to verify.

  • Summarise this document into five bullet points
  • Reformat this spreadsheet data into a table
  • Draft a follow-up email based on these meeting notes
  • Translate this paragraph into plain language
When it works best:

The task is routine, the instruction is specific, and you can check the output quickly. You're saving time on work you already know how to do.

When it doesn't:

The task is ambiguous, requires nuance, or you wouldn't recognise a wrong answer. Automation with no verification is just outsourcing your mistakes.

Mode 2: Augmentation (Creative Collaboration) "Think with me."

You and the AI work together as thinking partners. You're not handing over a task — you're having a conversation. The AI pushes your thinking, offers alternatives, asks questions, and helps you develop something you couldn't have reached as quickly on your own.

  • I'm planning a Year 8 unit on climate change — what angles could I take?
  • Here's my draft strategy — what am I missing?
  • I'm stuck on how to structure this report — talk me through some options
  • Challenge my assumptions about this approach
When it works best:

The task is complex, open-ended, or creative. You have the expertise to evaluate what comes back, and you're using the AI to extend your thinking rather than replace it.

When it doesn't:

You're looking for a definitive answer rather than a conversation. Augmentation requires you to bring judgment — if you defer to whatever the AI suggests, you've slipped back into automation without the verification.

Mode 3: Agency (Independent Action) "Act on my behalf."

The AI works more independently, making decisions within boundaries you've set. Instead of giving it one instruction at a time, you set the direction and let it run — more like a director giving a brief to a team than a scriptwriter dictating every line.

  • Monitor my inbox and categorise incoming emails by urgency
  • Research these five companies and produce a comparison brief
  • Build a first draft of this website based on this specification
When it works best:

You have a clear brief, well-defined boundaries, and a review process before anything goes live. You trust the tool enough to give it room, but you still check before it reaches anyone else.

When it doesn't:

The boundaries are unclear, there's no review step, or the consequences of a bad decision are significant. Giving AI that much independence without oversight is the highest-risk approach — it will proceed with full assurance regardless of whether it's getting things right.

How you actually interact: chat vs agents

In practice, the three modes map to two different ways of working with AI.

Chat (prompts in a conversation)

This is what most people start with. You open a tool like ChatGPT, Claude, or Copilot, type a prompt, read the response, and follow up. It's a conversation — back and forth, one message at a time. You're in control of every step.

Automation and augmentation mostly happen here. You write a prompt, the AI responds, you refine. The skill is in how you write the prompt and how you evaluate what comes back.

Agents (system instructions and tools)

This is how agency works in practice. Instead of typing one prompt at a time, you write a standing set of instructions — a brief that stays active the whole time, telling the AI who it is, how to behave, what it can access, and what rules to follow. Think of it as the difference between giving someone directions one turn at a time versus handing them a map and a destination.

An agent might have access to tools — your email, a database, a calendar, a file system — and make decisions about how to use them within the boundaries you've set.

The Anthropic training describes this as the shift from Scriptwriter to Director. In chat, you're the scriptwriter — you dictate every line. With agents, you're the director — you set the direction and the boundaries, then let the AI work within them. Your job changes from writing every prompt to setting the rules and checking the results.

Where most people are today

Most people are in chat. That's the right place to start — it builds the foundational skills (clear instructions, critical evaluation, iterative refinement) that you'll need before moving to agents. Giving AI that kind of independence without those skills is just unsupervised automation, and that's where things go wrong.

How the three modes relate

Most people start with automation — specific tasks, clear instructions, quick verification. As confidence and skill grow, they move into augmentation — using AI as a thinking partner for more complex work. Agency is where things are heading, but most people aren't there yet — it takes the right tools, clear rules, and a willingness to check the output.

You don't need to pick one mode. In a single working session, you might automate a formatting task, augment your planning, and then go back to automation for the next step. The skill is matching the mode to the task.

Key Landing Point

These three modes are a practical framework for deciding how to engage with AI once you've decided to use it. Automation is the entry point. Augmentation is where the real value lives for most professional work. Agency is where things are heading — but only with the right guardrails.
