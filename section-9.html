Advanced Prompting

Purpose: Once the basics are in place, these techniques take prompting further — particularly for complex, high-stakes, or multi-step work. Drawn from GovHub’s advanced prompting materials developed for Te Ara Ahunga Ora Retirement Commission.

Technique 1

Multi-stage prompting

Section 8 covered breaking a single prompt into steps. Multi-stage prompting goes further — it’s about breaking the entire task into separate prompts, each building on the last, with a human review checkpoint between stages. Break the work into stages, where each stage builds on the output of the previous one. Review the output at each stage before moving to the next.

A typical sequence: scan → pull out the key points → compare → draft → critique.

Example — a two-part approach
  • Part 1: “Analyse this document across these three dimensions. Output a table of findings.”
  • Review the table. Then Part 2: “Using the table you just produced, write a 150-word executive summary of the key findings for a senior audience.”

The principle: build in stages, review at each stage, don’t let the AI run the full pipeline unsupervised. Each stage is a checkpoint where you verify before moving on.

Technique 2

Iteration as a habit

Treat the first response as a draft, not a final product. Have a conversation. Push back. Ask for revisions. Tell it what’s wrong and what you want changed. The best results almost never come from the first prompt — they come from the third or fourth exchange.

Useful follow-ups
  • “That’s too long. Cut it to half the length and keep only the strongest points.”
  • “The tone is too formal. Rewrite as if you’re explaining this to a colleague over coffee.”
  • “You missed [specific thing]. Add it and restructure around it.”
  • “That’s good. Now challenge your own argument — what’s the strongest counterpoint?”

What separates good from great is knowing what to ask for next, not just what to ask for first.

Technique 3

Constraints and guardrails

Tell the AI what not to do. Think of these as safety rails. This is especially important for high-stakes work where errors carry consequences.

Useful constraints
  • “Do not fabricate sources or references.”
  • “Highlight any uncertainties or assumptions you’ve made.”
  • “Flag areas that require human review before this goes further.”
  • “If you don’t have enough information to answer confidently, say so.”
  • “Do not include personal opinions or speculative claims.”

They don’t guarantee the AI will follow them perfectly, but they significantly reduce the chance of confident-sounding errors making it through to your final output.

Technique 4

The diagnostic habit

Apply the same principle from Section 8: when output is weak, diagnose what’s missing rather than starting from scratch. The GCSE prompt framework (Goal and Role, Context, Source, Expectation) from Microsoft’s Copilot guidance. The diagnostic rule of thumb: when output is weak, ask which letter is missing.

G — Goal unclear

The AI didn’t know what you wanted or what role to adopt. Sharpen the action verb and be specific.

C — Context missing

It didn’t have enough background to tailor the response. Add the who, why, and situation.

S — Source not specified

It guessed instead of using the material you wanted. Point it to specific content.

E — Expectations vague

You didn’t say what the output should look like. Specify format, tone, length, and structure.

Fix the weak element. Leave the rest. This is faster and more effective than rewriting the entire prompt.

Technique 5

Ask the AI to improve your approach

Section 8 introduced the idea of asking the AI to help you write a prompt. At the advanced level, this becomes a planning tool for complex multi-step work:

Examples
  • “I need to analyse these three policy documents. What would be the most effective sequence of prompts to do this thoroughly?”
  • “I’ve written this prompt but the output isn’t what I need. What’s wrong with my prompt and how would you improve it?”
  • “Suggest three ways I could break this task down into smaller steps.”

This turns the AI into a collaborator on the process, not just the content.

Key Landing Point

Basic prompting gets you started. Advanced prompting is where the real productivity gains live — particularly for complex, multi-step, or high-stakes work. The common thread is intentionality: plan before you prompt, review before you proceed, and always keep the human in the loop at every stage.
