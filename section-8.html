Prompting Essentials

Purpose: The practical how-to for writing effective prompts. Section 7 introduced Description as a core skill — this section gives people the techniques to actually do it. Drawn from Anthropic’s “6 Techniques for Effective Prompt Engineering.”

Six techniques that make the difference between vague output and useful output:

Technique 1

Provide context

Don’t just say what you want — say why, for whom, and in what situation. The more relevant background you provide, the more useful the response.

Weak

“Tell me about climate change.”

Stronger

“Explain three major impacts of climate change on agriculture in tropical regions, with examples from the past decade.”

Without your context, the AI is guessing. This is how you close that gap.

Technique 2

Show examples of what “good” looks like

If you want the AI to produce something in a particular style, format, or register, show it an example first. Give it one or two examples of the pattern you want, then ask it to follow that pattern. (AI people call this “few-shot prompting,” but it’s really just showing the AI what good looks like before asking it to have a go.)

Weak

“Convert this technical statement to plain language.”

Stronger

“Here are two examples of how to convert technical jargon to plain language: [example 1] [example 2]. Now convert this statement using the same approach.”

Showing beats telling. An example does more than a paragraph of instructions.

Technique 3

Specify output constraints

Tell it exactly what you want the output to look like — length, format, structure, tone. If you don’t specify, you get whatever the AI’s default is, which may not be what you need.

Weak

“Design me a portfolio website.”

Stronger

“Create a clean, single-page portfolio with these sections: About Me, Projects, Experience, Contact. Keep the language professional and concise.”

Be specific about scope, format, length, and any parameters that matter.

Technique 4

Break complex tasks into steps

Don’t try to do everything in one prompt. If the task has multiple parts, lay them out as numbered steps. This guides the AI’s reasoning and produces more thorough, structured responses.

Weak

“Analyse this quarterly sales data.”

Stronger

“Analyse this quarterly sales data by: 1. Identifying top-performing products, 2. Comparing this quarter to the previous quarter, 3. Highlighting unusual patterns or trends, 4. Suggesting possible reasons for those trends.”

Breaking it down guides the AI’s reasoning process and ensures it doesn’t skip parts.

Technique 5

Ask it to think first

Before the AI gives you an answer, ask it to consider the problem carefully — think through factors, constraints, and approaches before recommending a solution. This produces more thoughtful, considered responses.

Example

“Before answering, think through this problem carefully. Consider the different factors involved, potential constraints, and various approaches before recommending the best solution.”

Giving the AI space to reason before responding encourages more comprehensive answers.

Technique 6

Define the AI’s role, style, or tone

Tell the AI who it should be for this task — what perspective, expertise, and audience it should have in mind. This shapes the tone, depth, and framing of the response.

Example

“Explain how rainbows form from the perspective of an experienced science teacher speaking to a bright 10-year-old who’s interested in science.”

Defining the role, tone, or style helps shape the AI’s approach to fit your specific needs and audience.

Secret weapon: ask the AI to help you write the prompt

If you’re not sure how to ask for something, ask the AI itself. “I’m trying to get you to help me with [goal]. I’m not sure how to phrase my request to get the best results. Can you help me craft an effective prompt for this?”

This is perhaps the most powerful technique of all — and the one most people overlook.

Key Landing Point

These six techniques aren’t complicated. They’re just habits of clear communication applied to a new medium. When output is weak, the fix is almost always one of these six — sharpen the context, add an example, constrain the output, break it into steps, ask it to think first, or define the role. Diagnose which one is missing rather than rewriting the entire prompt from scratch.
