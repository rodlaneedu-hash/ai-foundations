What is AI?

Purpose: Establish what we're actually talking about, so people stop conflating different things under "AI."

Four key distinctions:

1

Artificial Intelligence (broad field) — any system designed to perform tasks that normally require human intelligence. This is a field of computer science, not a single technology.

2

Narrow AI — AI trained for a specific task. This is what exists today and what people interact with daily: spam filters, voice assistants, the suggestions Netflix gives you, predictive text. It does one thing well. It doesn't "think."

3

General AI (AGI) — a hypothetical machine that can perform any intellectual task a human can. This is the sci-fi version — Terminator, HAL 9000, sentient robots. It does not exist. Nobody serious can tell you when — or if — that will happen.

4

Generative AI — a subset of narrow AI that creates new content (text, images, code, audio, video). This is ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, Midjourney. This is what the rest of this document is about.

Key Landing Point

When people say "AI" they usually mean one of these four things without realising they're different. Generative AI is powerful and useful, but it is narrow AI — it's not going to wake up and become conscious. It does not understand, and it cannot do everything. That distinction matters for every decision you'll make about how to use it.

A Note on the Tools Available

There isn't just one AI tool out there. ChatGPT (from OpenAI), Claude (from Anthropic), Gemini (from Google), Copilot (from Microsoft), and others all work on the same underlying principles, but they have different strengths, different limitations, and different policies around data and privacy. Part of using AI well is choosing the right tool for the task — and checking the terms of the one you use. The tools change fast. No comparison table stays accurate for long.
